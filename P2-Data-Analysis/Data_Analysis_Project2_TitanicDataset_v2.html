1. Introduction¶

RMS Titanic was a British passenger liner that sank in the North Atlantic Ocean in the early morning of 15 April 1912, after colliding with an iceberg during her maiden voyage from Southampton to New York City. Of the estimated 2,224 passengers and crew aboard, more than 1,500 died, making it one of the deadliest commercial peacetime maritime disasters in modern history.

One of the reasosns that led to the loss of life was the lack of enough lifeboats. Although some amount of luck was involved in surviving the disaster, in this project we analyse some of the factors that may have helped some passengers survive and others not.

Test the hypothesis - There is a relationshio between passenger class and survival

The 2 variables we are interested in studying are Pclass and Survived. Both Pclass and Survived are categorical variables. In order to determine if there is a significant association between these 2 variables, we will use a Chi-Square Test for Independence.

For the purposes of this test, we assume that the Titanic data is a random sample.

  • Null hypothesis, Ho: Passenger class and Survival are independent
  • Alternate hypothesis, Ha: Passenger class and Survival are not independent

For this analysis, the significant level is 0.05.

In [12]:
# Use the chi square method from scipy.stats to calculate the chi square value,P-value, degrees of freedom and expected frequency
chi2,p,dof,expected = chi2_contingency(survival_by_class,correction=False)
print 'p-value: ', p

p-value:  6.70986174976e-21

P value 6.70986174976e-21 is lesser than the significance level 0.05. Hence we reject the null hypothesis. There is a relationship between Passenger class and Survival.

4.4 Did age impact survival?¶

We already saw in 4.2 that women had a better chance of survival than men. Logic would suggest that women and children would have been rescued first. Let us see if this was the case.

We will use a violin plot to see the distribution of survival across ages for male and female passengers in each class.

A violin plot is similar to a box plot and is useful to see the distribution of data across one or more categorical variables such that they can be compared. Unlike a boxplot, a violin plot features the kernel density estimation instead of actual datapooint.

In [20]:
# A violin plot with Age, Sex and Survived plotted horizontally with one Pclass on each row
f = sns.factorplot('Age','Sex',hue='Survived',palette='Set3',hue_order=['Survived','Died'],
                   row='Pclass', orient ='h',size=2,aspect =3.5,
                   data=titanic_no_missing, kind='violin',split=True, cut=0, bw=.2)

We can see that in classes 1 and 2, females and children seem to have been rescued first (with the exception of few in class 1). In class 3 this does not seem to be the case.

We do not see real trends for the impact of age on survival.

Hence we can conclude that age has an impact on survival, but the impact does not seem to be significant.

5. Conclusion¶

4 variables were analyzed for impact on survival rate:

  1. Travel Class
  2. Sex
  3. Family onboard
  4. Age

In conclusion, let's look at one more visual, a heatmap that shows the correlation value for the above 5 variables.

In [22]:
# Create a subset with the fields of interest

titanic_subset = titanic_no_missing.filter(['Pclass','Sex','Family','Age','Survived'], axis=1)

#Convert sex to a numerical value, male=0,female=1 so that it can be included in the correlation heatmap
titanic_subset.loc[:,['Sex']]  = titanic_subset.loc[:,['Sex']].replace(['male','female'],[0,1])

#Convert Survived to a numerical value, died=0,survived=1 so that it can be included in the correlation heatmap
titanic_subset.loc[:,['Survived']]  = titanic_subset.loc[:,['Survived']].replace(['Died','Survived'],[0,1])

#Plot the heatmap
ax = sns.heatmap(titanic_subset.corr(),annot=True,linewidths=0.5,cmap='BuGn')

  • Passengers in Class1 have a better chance of survival than Class2 and Class 2 passengers have a better chance of survival than Class3.

  • Women are more likely to survive than men.

  • Age has a minor impact on the survival rate.

  • Travelling with family did not have a significant impact on survival.

Limitations:

  • The dataset contains information for only 891 of the 2022 passengers and crew onboard. Furthermore, 177 more records with no age information were not considered in the analyis.
  • The data does not contain information on cabin crew vs passengers and cabin information is missing for most passenger data. These factors could have impacted survival and could also have impacted some of the results that were arrived at in this analysis.
